Right, I mean that's why you don't start the game with 50% wolves unless there's something really interesting going on. But the wolves actually had a great deal to figure out: who the likely roles were, which players they could safely target for night kills without backlash, which players could be safely targeted for lynch without risk of claiming a particularly believable role. In a very particular instance: we chose to night kill mystic because of his response to narwhal's claim. We didn't KNOW that he was the guardian, but we needed to patch that sort of information together to decide who was the best kill. Also, death butt, your guardian claim would have been entirely unconvincing because of your response to narwhal, and would probably have only worked to wolf advantage.

The thing is, we, as wolves, did a pretty good job figuring out which players had which roles based on clues dropped in the thread, better than any other game I've played as a wolf. We guessed that modi might be something like bulletproof because he was so comfortable saying that he had a role, but not specifying what it was. We guessed that reaper had some sort of role, more based on gut feeling than anything else. If you look at the roles we knocked out with out night kills, we were hitting well enough that, I think, a balanced game would have at least leveled the playing ground against a few of the mistakes that, say, narwhal made.

If we had chosen our nightkills poorly, however (for instance, targetting dietcar n1), and if iron and reaper had gotten lucky, the ENTIRE WOLF TEAM could have been revealed by d2. And our one power, roleblocking, ended up being used against us by more or less confirming the masons. There was the potential of a huge, rapid confirmation of players that we couldn't really have done anything against.

So, I disagree with the idea that wolves have nothing to figure out, and I think the distribution/function of human roles significantly reduced the amount that the humans needed to figure out.

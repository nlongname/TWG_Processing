Did it go so badly? Under what conditions would the spies see the mission as problematic?

I am working, fundamentally, under the assumption: A spy would consider a mission disadvantageous to their cause if it does not contain any spies.

Perhaps secondarily: A mission is suboptimal if it contains more spies than is needed to sabotage the mission. (though it carries with it the possible benefit of "confirming" a spy, it carries the risk of eliminating a number of the spies from future missions)

In this first, off the cuff mission, a spy would have ample reason to vote against a spy-free team without drawing attention to the fact that they are doing so based on the first reason. They did not, however, do so.

In the second, I imagine there would be mixed sentiments regarding how to respond to the mission.

We also know that Phil claimed (albeit jokingly) that he was going to vote against the team, yesterday, and that he did not do so today. Today, instead, he pointed out the pseudo-confirmed benefit of a spy voting for a mission to succeed (and thereby be put on a future team again).

As a side note, I pointed out Phil as a likely spy candidate before the results of the mission were submitted, which may have put some pressure on him to try to seem "confirmed" through the mission results.

  • Quote
The spies cannot talk to one another outside the thread, so if eep is a spy, it is likely that the other spies did not agree with her no vote. So that may be one of the reasons why eep was the only no vote. This game requires a lot of psychological understanding. We all basically are guessing at the choices others will make.  We don't even really get to see what is voted for until after it is all voted for.

There are basically four possibilities for this first group.

1) three resistance
2) two resistance and one spy
3) two spies and one resistance
4) three spies

If they were all spies, there is a very good possibility that there would be at least one vote in there for sabotage. But it could also go the opposite way, all of them might have been thinking that the other would make the choice for sabotage and led the the bystander effect of no one voting for sabotage.

If there were two spies, the above case of bystander effect may also have been a part of the decision. Also, if there were two spies in this group, and they were both to vote for sabotage, then those three players would most likely not be put into any more successful groups, and therefore would be unable to vote in future situations. It actually would be better for them to just let this one time go by with no sabotage.

If there was one spy they may have decided to just let this time slide because then no one would be pointed out as a spy yet.

If there were no spies, then it is really obvious why there was no sabotage.

Those are my views on what is happening so far.

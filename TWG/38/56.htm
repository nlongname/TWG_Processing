Phil-human-seer gets a big advantage from claiming because he outed a wolf. That one is obvious.

Phil-wolf-seer gets a big advantage because he kills two or more known guards at the cost of only one wolf.

Phil-wolf-vanilla gets a big advantage because he kills one known guard (deathbutt) and/or determines the identity of me when I confess I'm a guard or disclaim him (in the latter case they still must NK deathbutt).

It's a Xanatos gambit - no matter if he is right or wrong, no matter his alignment or role, wolves come out ahead.



Average, lynching no guard claimaints, assuming we have a little less than 2 average wolves.
D3 (Today): We have 7 maybes.
Lynch a nonguard.
Begin N3: 6 left, 3 guard, 1.5 wolves, 1.5 humans.
Lose 0.66 of a guard.
Begin D4: 5 left. We have 2.3 guard, 1.5 wolves, 1.2 humans.
Lynch a nonguard.
Begin N4. 4 left. We have 2.3 guards, 1 wolf, 0.7 humans.
Lose 0.7 of a guard.
Begin D5: 3 left. We have 1.5 guards, 1 wolf, .5 humans.
Lynch a nonguard.
Begin N5: 2 left. We have 1.5 guards, 0.3 wolves, 0.2 humans
Begin D6: 1 left + eep
Probability of Eep being alive: 100%
Probability of a single guard being alive: 60%
Probability of a wolf being alive: 30%
Probability of an additional human being alive: 10%


As you can see, the less guards there are, the better the wolves are doing. This also generously assumes we've killed a wolf.

 Our biggest advantage is delaying the killing of a guard the longest we can afford to, until we really think we have a guard-claimant wolf. This should be obvious even without numbers, objectively (which is more what I mean by mathematically, I suppose).

Even if you don't like the probability thing, you can still see from DB's decision trees that similar things will go down.

TL;DR - More guards = longer survival = more chance of killing wolves.

It makes SENSE to kill wolves that are not masquerading as guards unless you are VERY CONFIDENT, and I don't see why you'd be so confident.
